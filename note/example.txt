World War II, also known as the Second World War, was a global conflict that lasted from 1939 to 1945. It involved many of the world's nations, organized into two opposing military alliances: the Allies and the Axis. It was the most widespread war in history, and it directly involved more than 100 million people from over 30 countries.

Key Dates and Locations:
The war officially began on September 1, 1939, when Germany, led by Adolf Hitler, invaded Poland. The conflict spread worldwide with the invasion of France, the bombing of Britain by Germany (known as the Battle of Britain), and the attacks on the Soviet Union. The war in the Pacific began on December 7, 1941, when Japan attacked the American fleet at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii.

Key Individuals:
Key figures during World War II include Adolf Hitler (Germany), Benito Mussolini (Italy), Emperor Hirohito (Japan) for the Axis Powers, and Winston Churchill (United Kingdom), Franklin D. Roosevelt (USA), and Joseph Stalin (Soviet Union) for the Allies.

Causes and Consequences:
The causes of World War II are rooted in the aggressive foreign policies of Nazi Germany and the political instability in the world following World War I, including the economic turmoil caused by the Great Depression in the 1930s. The consequences of the war were far-reaching. It resulted in the death of an estimated 70-85 million people, approximately 3-4% of the world's population at the time. It led to major changes in the world's political structure, including the emergence of the United States and the Soviet Union as superpowers and the beginning of the Cold War.

Historical Context and Broader Themes:
World War II marked a turning point in world history. It led to a reconfiguration of the global balance of power, the decolonization of Africa and Asia, and the formation of the United Nations. The war also saw significant advancements in technology and warfare, the horrors of the Holocaust, and the only use of nuclear weapons in warfare to date.
